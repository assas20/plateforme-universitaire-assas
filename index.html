Exercise 01: Vector defined by two points

Given: Points \(A(2, 3, -3)\) and \(B(5, 7, 2)\).

Definition: Components and Magnitude of a vector

A vector \(\vec{AB}\) is defined by the difference in the coordinates of its endpoint (B) and starting point (A). Its magnitude (or norm), denoted \(||\vec{AB}||\), represents its length and is calculated by extending the Pythagorean theorem to three dimensions.

Step 1: Calculating the components

The components of the vector \(\vec{AB}\) are obtained as follows:

\[ \vec{AB} = (x_B - x_A)\vec{i} + (y_B - y_A)\vec{j} + (z_B - z_A)\vec{k} \]
\[ \vec{AB} = (5 - 2)\vec{i} + (7 - 3)\vec{j} + (2 - (-3))\vec{k} = 3\vec{i} + 4\vec{j} + 5\vec{k} \]

Step 2: Calculating the magnitude (Norm)

The magnitude is the square root of the sum of the squares of its components.

\[ ||\vec{AB}|| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16 + 25} = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2} \approx 7.07 \]

Step 3: Determining the direction (Direction Cosines)

The direction is defined by the angles \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) that the vector forms with the Ox, Oy, Oz axes. These angles are found via the direction cosines.

\[ \cos \alpha = \frac{AB_x}{||\vec{AB}||} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.424 \implies \alpha \approx 64.9^\circ \]
\[ \cos \beta = \frac{AB_y}{||\vec{AB}||} = \frac{4}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.566 \implies \beta \approx 55.5^\circ \]
\[ \cos \gamma = \frac{AB_z}{||\vec{AB}||} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.707 \implies \gamma \approx 45.0^\circ \]

Step 4: Determining the sense

The sense of the vector with respect to the axes is given by the sign of its components. Here, all components (3, 4, 5) are positive, so the vector is oriented in the positive direction of all three axes.

Exercise 02: Operations on vectors

Given: \(\vec{u} = \begin{pmatrix} -8 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -9 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\).

1. Magnitudes of the vectors

\[ ||\vec{u}|| = \sqrt{(-8)^2 + 2^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{64 + 4 + 25} = \sqrt{93} \approx 9.64 \]
\[ ||\vec{v}|| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-9)^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 81 + 1} = \sqrt{91} \approx 9.54 \]

2. Scalar product

The scalar product is the sum of the products of the corresponding components.

\[ \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = (-8)(3) + (2)(-9) + (5)(-1) = -24 - 18 - 5 = -47 \]

3. Vector product

The vector product is calculated using a formal determinant.

\[ \vec{u} \wedge \vec{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k} \\ -8 & 2 & 5 \\ 3 & -9 & -1 \end{vmatrix} = \vec{i}(2(-1) - 5(-9)) - \vec{j}((-8)(-1) - 5(3)) + \vec{k}((-8)(-9) - 2(3)) \]
\[ = 43\vec{i} + 7\vec{j} + 66\vec{k} \]

4. Direction cosines of the unit vectors

The direction cosines of a vector are the components of its unit vector.

For \(\vec{u}\) : \(\hat{u} = \frac{\vec{u}}{||\vec{u}||} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{93}}(-8\vec{i} + 2\vec{j} + 5\vec{k})\).

  • \(\cos \alpha_u = -8/\sqrt{93}\)
  • \(\cos \beta_u = 2/\sqrt{93}\)
  • \(\cos \gamma_u = 5/\sqrt{93}\)

For \(\vec{v}\) : \(\hat{v} = \frac{\vec{v}}{||\vec{v}||} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{91}}(3\vec{i} - 9\vec{j} - \vec{k})\).

  • \(\cos \alpha_v = 3/\sqrt{91}\)
  • \(\cos \beta_v = -9/\sqrt{91}\)
  • \(\cos \gamma_v = -1/\sqrt{91}\)

Exercise 03: Collinearity and Perpendicularity

Given: \(\vec{V}_1 = \vec{i} + 4\vec{k}\), \(\vec{V}_2 = 2\vec{i} + y\vec{j} + z\vec{k}\), \(\vec{V}_3 = \vec{i} - 2\vec{j} + 4\vec{k}\), \(\vec{V}_4 = 4\vec{i} + y\vec{j} + 2\vec{k}\).

1. Condition for collinearity

Two vectors are collinear if their components are proportional, i.e., \(\vec{V}_2 = k \vec{V}_1\) for some scalar \(k\).

\[ \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = k \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \implies \begin{cases} 2 = k \cdot 1 \\ y = k \cdot 0 \\ z = k \cdot 4 \end{cases} \]

From the first equation, \(k=2\). Substituting into the others, we find \(y = 2 \cdot 0 = 0\) and \(z = 2 \cdot 4 = 8\).

2. Condition for perpendicularity

Two vectors are perpendicular (orthogonal) if their scalar product is zero (\(\vec{V}_3 \cdot \vec{V}_4 = 0\)).

\[ (\vec{i} - 2\vec{j} + 4\vec{k}) \cdot (4\vec{i} + y\vec{j} + 2\vec{k}) = 0 \]
\[ (1)(4) + (-2)(y) + (4)(2) = 0 \implies 4 - 2y + 8 = 0 \implies 12 = 2y \implies y = 6 \]

Exercise 04: Unit vector application

Given: \(A(3, 7, -2)\), \(B(0, 5, 1)\), \(C(-2, 2, 4)\). Find point \(D(x, y, z)\) such that \(\vec{CD}\) is the unit vector of \(\vec{AB}\).

Step 1: Calculate the vector \(\vec{AB}\) and its magnitude

\[ \vec{AB} = (0-3)\vec{i} + (5-7)\vec{j} + (1-(-2))\vec{k} = -3\vec{i} - 2\vec{j} + 3\vec{k} \]
\[ ||\vec{AB}|| = \sqrt{(-3)^2 + (-2)^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{9+4+9} = \sqrt{22} \]

Step 2: Determine the unit vector of \(\vec{AB}\)

\[ \hat{u}_{AB} = \frac{\vec{AB}}{||\vec{AB}||} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{22}}(-3\vec{i} - 2\vec{j} + 3\vec{k}) \]

Step 3: Solve for point D

We set \(\vec{CD} = \hat{u}_{AB}\). The vector \(\vec{CD}\) is expressed as \((x - (-2))\vec{i} + (y - 2)\vec{j} + (z - 4)\vec{k}\).

\[ (x+2)\vec{i} + (y-2)\vec{j} + (z-4)\vec{k} = -\frac{3}{\sqrt{22}}\vec{i} - \frac{2}{\sqrt{22}}\vec{j} + \frac{3}{\sqrt{22}}\vec{k} \]

By equating the components:

\[ \begin{cases} x+2 = -3/\sqrt{22} \implies x = -2 - 3/\sqrt{22} \\ y-2 = -2/\sqrt{22} \implies y = 2 - 2/\sqrt{22} \\ z-4 = 3/\sqrt{22} \implies z = 4 + 3/\sqrt{22} \end{cases} \]

The coordinates of point D are \( D(-2 - \frac{3}{\sqrt{22}}, 2 - \frac{2}{\sqrt{22}}, 4 + \frac{3}{\sqrt{22}}) \).

Exercise 05: Derivative of vector products

    \(\deriv(56t^4) = 224t^3\).

  • Right-hand side (RHS):

    \(\deriv{\vec{P}} = 2\vec{i} + 10t\vec{j} - 21t^2\vec{k}\) and \(\deriv{\vec{Q}} = -12t^2\vec{i} + 20t\vec{j} - 2\vec{k}\).

    \(\deriv{\vec{P}} \cdot \vec{Q} = (2)(-4t^3) + (10t)(10t^2) + (-21t^2)(-2t) = -8t^3 + 100t^3 + 42t^3 = 134t^3\).

    \(\vec{P} \cdot \deriv{\vec{Q}} = (2t)(-12t^2) + (5t^2)(20t) + (-7t^3)(-2) = -24t^3 + 100t^3 + 14t^3 = 90t^3\).

    RHS = \(134t^3 + 90t^3 = 224t^3\).

LHS = RHS, the relationship is verified.

  • Left-hand side (LHS):

    \(\deriv(\vec{P} \wedge \vec{Q}) = (350t^4 - 30t^2)\vec{i} + (168t^5 + 8t)\vec{j} + (100t^4 + 60t^2)\vec{k}\).

  • Right-hand side (RHS):

    \(\deriv{\vec{P}} = 2\vec{i} + 10t\vec{j} - 21t^2\vec{k}\).

    \(\deriv{\vec{Q}} = -12t^2\vec{i} + 20t\vec{j} - 2\vec{k}\).

    \(\deriv{\vec{P}} \wedge \vec{Q} = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k} \\ 2 & 10t & -21t^2 \\ -4t^3 & 10t^2 & -2t \end{vmatrix} = (210t^4 - 20t^2)\vec{i} + (84t^5 + 4t)\vec{j} + (40t^4 + 20t^2)\vec{k}\).

    \(\vec{P} \wedge \deriv{\vec{Q}} = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k} \\ 2t & 5t^2 & -7t^3 \\ -12t^2 & 20t & -2 \end{vmatrix} = (140t^4 - 10t^2)\vec{i} + (84t^5 + 4t)\vec{j} + (60t^4 + 40t^2)\vec{k}\).

    RHS = \( (210t^4 - 20t^2 + 140t^4 - 10t^2)\vec{i} + (84t^5 + 4t + 84t^5 + 4t)\vec{j} + (40t^4 + 20t^2 + 60t^4 + 40t^2)\vec{k}\)

    RHS = \( (350t^4 - 30t^2)\vec{i} + (168t^5 + 8t)\vec{j} + (100t^4 + 60t^2)\vec{k}\).
